This Place Was Always Different

Taiwan's Landscapes That Carry Something Else
Lu Lin  ·  Still Time Corner
Preview Edition
Preface

The Island That Remembers

Taiwan sits where two tectonic plates are slowly eating each other. The Philippine Sea Plate pushes west into the Eurasian Plate at a rate of about eight centimeters per year — fast, by geological standards. The island is being actively built and destroyed at the same time. Mountains rise and rivers cut through them. Cliffs collapse into the Pacific. The ground shakes, not occasionally, but constantly: Taiwan experiences hundreds of perceptible earthquakes every year, and thousands more too small for human bodies to register.

This is not a metaphor. But it is also not only geology.

To understand why certain places in Taiwan feel different — heavier, quieter, wrong in a way you can't name — you need to start with the land itself. Because the people who have lived here across four centuries haven't just been building culture on top of neutral terrain. They've been building it on top of a landscape that regularly swallows roads, collapses coastlines, and buries villages under mudslides. The land participates in history here. It always has.

Before we talk about temples and ghost months and mountain roads that locals refuse to drive at night, you need to know what has happened on this island. Not as background. As the thing itself.

Four Hundred Years of Arrivals

The Austronesian peoples who became Taiwan's indigenous nations — the Atayal, the Paiwan, the Amis, the Bunun, and at least thirteen other distinct groups — had been living here for thousands of years before anyone else arrived. They had mapped the island in ways that didn't involve cartography: through oral tradition, through clan territories, through the sacred boundaries between the living world and the spirit world that every community understood and maintained.

Then, in 1624, the Dutch arrived and built Fort Zeelandia on the southwestern coast near present-day Tainan. The Spanish built their own fort in the north two years later. Neither stayed long. In 1662, the Ming loyalist general Koxinga (Zheng Chenggong) expelled the Dutch and used Taiwan as a base of resistance against the Qing Dynasty that had taken mainland China. The Qing eventually took Taiwan anyway, in 1683, and governed it — loosely, ambivalently — for the next two hundred years.

In 1895, Taiwan was ceded to Japan following the First Sino-Japanese War. The Japanese colonial period lasted fifty years and left deep marks: infrastructure, education systems, architectural styles, agricultural engineering, and a comprehensive campaign to suppress and reorganize indigenous cultures. In 1945, Japan surrendered and Taiwan came under the Republic of China government, which was itself fleeing the mainland after losing the civil war to the Communist Party in 1949. Roughly 1.2 million soldiers and civilians crossed the strait and settled across the island, bringing their own traditions, dialects, and traumas.

Each of these arrivals layered something onto Taiwan's cultural landscape. And each of them also left behind people who died — violently, suddenly, in unfamiliar ground, far from their ancestral homes. In Taiwanese religious understanding, the manner of death and the location of death matter enormously. Someone who dies suddenly and far from family, without proper rites, becomes an unmoored spirit. Multiply that by centuries of war, displacement, epidemic, and industrial accident, and you begin to understand why certain places here feel populated in ways that don't show up on any census.

What "Different" Means Here

Taiwanese people don't usually use the word "haunted." The vocabulary is more nuanced than that. A place might be described as 不乾淨 (bù gānjìng) — "not clean." Or there might be 好兄弟 (hǎo xiōngdì) — literally "good brothers," a polite euphemism for spirits of those who died without family to perform the proper rites. Or a location might simply be said to have 煞氣 (shà qì) — a kind of violent or destructive energy associated with sudden death or trauma.

These aren't horror-movie concepts. They're frameworks for managing a genuine cultural reality: that the dead require ongoing relationships with the living, and that places carry the memory of what has happened in them. When Taiwanese people say a road is 不乾淨, they're not necessarily claiming supernatural agency. They're making a statement about the accumulated weight of that location's history — and recommending appropriate caution.

This book is about five categories of places where that weight becomes palpable. Mountain roads. Temples for the unworshipped dead. Old neighborhoods where certain buildings refuse to sell. Rivers and coastlines with specific, named dangers. And abandoned structures where history compressed itself into ruin.

In each chapter, I'll give you the cultural explanation before I give you the atmospheric description. Because that's the only honest way to do this. The uncanniness of these places isn't decoration. It's a direct product of what has happened on this island, and who has been forgotten in the process.

Chapter One

The Mountain Roads That Locals Don't Drive at Night

There are two road trips that Taiwanese people will warn you about before you attempt them after dark. One is the North-Yi Highway — 北宜公路 (Běiyí Gōnglù), Provincial Highway 9 — a 35-kilometer stretch of mountain road connecting Taipei to Yilan that winds through the central ridge of northern Taiwan. The other is the Suhua Highway — 蘇花公路 (Sūhuā Gōnglù), Provincial Highway 9C — a 118-kilometer coastal cliff road hugging the edge of the Qingshui Cliffs (清水斷崖) between Suao and Hualien, where in many places the mountain rises straight up on one side and the Pacific drops straight down on the other.

Both roads have genuine, documentable histories of fatal accidents. The North-Yi Highway's extreme curves — 376 bends in 35 kilometers — have claimed hundreds of lives since the road was fully paved in the 1950s. The Suhua has seen regular landslides, some of which have swept vehicles directly off the cliff edge. These are not abstract dangers. They are statistical realities that any Taiwanese person who drives regularly has internalized.

But the warnings you'll hear aren't primarily about statistics. They're about something older than that.

What the Atayal Know About Mountains

The northern mountain ranges that the North-Yi Highway cuts through are ancestral territory of the Atayal people (泰雅族, Tàiyǎ Zú), one of Taiwan's sixteen officially recognized indigenous peoples. Before the Japanese colonial government built roads through these ranges and before Han Chinese settlers pushed into the highlands, the Atayal had been living in these mountains for approximately eight thousand years.

The Atayal worldview does not separate the natural and supernatural worlds. Mountains are not scenery. They are inhabited — by the spirits of ancestors, by Utux (the Atayal term for spirits or souls, roughly), and by forces that require acknowledgment and respect. Certain peaks, certain valleys, certain river crossings carry specific spiritual significance. There are places you enter only with the right preparation. There are places you don't enter at all.

In traditional Atayal practice, before passing through mountain territory — especially in areas associated with death, whether from warfare, accident, or illness — a traveler would perform specific ritual acknowledgments. These weren't superstition. They were a form of social protocol extended to the non-human residents of a place: an announcement of your presence, a statement of peaceful intent, and a recognition that you were a guest in a landscape that had its own inhabitants and its own logic.

The Atayal concept of gaga — a term that encompasses ancestral rules, sacred prohibitions, and the moral order that holds community life together — includes specific protocols for engaging with forests, rivers, and mountains. Violation of gaga doesn't just bring bad luck in a casual sense. It represents a rupture in the relationship between the living, the dead, and the land itself. Scholars of Atayal culture, including anthropologist Mona Rudao's contemporary descendants who have published on traditional knowledge, describe gaga as something closer to constitutional law than to folk belief.

— Documented in research on Atayal traditional ecological knowledge, Council of Indigenous Peoples, Taiwan

When the Japanese and later ROC governments built roads through these mountains without ceremony, without acknowledgment, often through sites that Atayal communities recognized as spiritually significant, they weren't just displacing people. From the Atayal perspective, they were violating the terms under which humans were permitted to occupy that landscape. The mountains didn't forget.

The North-Yi Highway's Specific History

The North-Yi Highway follows a route that was originally a Japanese colonial-era trail, later expanded into a road suitable for vehicles in the post-war period. Its 376 curves aren't arbitrary engineering — they trace the most passable contours of an extremely steep mountain terrain. The road is genuinely difficult to drive even in daylight, in good weather, by an experienced driver.

Its accident history is extensive and traceable. Local news archives and traffic authority records document fatal accidents occurring on this road across multiple decades. The bend at Jiuwan (九彎), near the Pinglin end of the highway, is particularly notorious — a section where the road curves so sharply and steeply that misjudging it, even slightly, means going over the edge.

What makes the North-Yi Highway interesting culturally is not the accident statistics, though. It's the way those accidents have been incorporated into the local spiritual landscape. The 好兄弟 — the "good brothers," the spirits of those who died suddenly and violently — are understood to linger near the sites of their deaths. On a road with as many fatal accidents as the North-Yi, this creates a dense accumulation. Drivers passing through the mountain at night often describe a particular quality of unease that they find difficult to articulate: a sense of being watched, or of the road feeling longer and more disorienting than it does in daylight.

The practical response to this is not to perform elaborate ritual. Most Taiwanese drivers who take the North-Yi at night do something simpler: they talk. Not necessarily to anyone specific. Just — they talk. Keep a conversation going with the car's other passengers, or speak aloud if they're alone. The silence of the mountain road at night is understood to be a kind of invitation, and filling it with ordinary human speech is understood to be a way of maintaining your own presence against the weight of the other presences around you.

This isn't about literal ghost-belief for most of the drivers who practice it. Ask them and many will shrug, or laugh a little, and say they don't really think anything will happen. But they keep talking anyway. That gap — between what a person consciously believes and what they do when alone on a dark mountain road at three in the morning — is where Taiwanese spiritual culture actually lives.

The Suhua Highway and the Sea Below

The Suhua Highway presents a different kind of accumulated weight. Where the North-Yi is inland mountain, the Suhua is coastal cliff — 118 kilometers of road carved into the face of the Qingshui Cliffs, which drop in some sections almost vertically to the Pacific, from heights of over one thousand meters.

The road's accident history includes not just vehicle crashes but natural disasters: typhoon-triggered landslides that have buried sections of the road under meters of rock, sometimes with vehicles and their occupants. The most catastrophic in recent memory was Typhoon Sinlaku in 2008, which triggered multiple landslides along the highway and disrupted access to Hualien for days. In 2010, Typhoon Megi caused a landslide that swept an entire tour bus off the cliff into the ocean. Twenty-six passengers died.

The Suhua runs through territory that includes both Atayal and Truku (太魯閣族, Tàilǔgé Zú) ancestral lands. The Truku, a related group whose traditional territory centers on what is now Taroko Gorge National Park, have their own protocols for engaging with cliffs, rivers, and the sea — all considered inhabited landscapes with their own spiritual logic. The Qingshui Cliffs in particular are understood in some Truku traditional narratives as a boundary zone: a place of transition between the navigable world and something beyond it.

Taiwanese truck drivers and bus drivers who regularly travel the Suhua describe a set of informal practices: slowing down near known accident sites, honking at blind curves not just for safety reasons but as a kind of announcement, and maintaining a particular quality of alertness that experienced drivers say feels qualitatively different from alertness on ordinary roads. One retired highway bus driver I spoke with described it as "driving with awareness of who else might be on the road with you" — and did not mean other cars.

Why Mountains Are Different Here

Taiwan's mountain roads carry this particular weight for reasons that go beyond their accident histories. Mountains in Taiwan have been contested terrain for the entire duration of documented history. They were where indigenous peoples retreated when lowland Taiwan was being colonized. They were where Japanese colonial administrators drew the boundary of their effective control — the Riban jichi system literally divided Taiwan into administrated and "non-administrated" territory, with the mountains falling largely outside direct governance. They were where Taiwanese resistance fighters hid. They were where political fugitives went.

The mountains have always been the place where the rules of the lowlands didn't fully apply. That dual status — refuge and danger, freedom and death — is embedded in how Taiwanese people relate to elevated terrain. When you drive the North-Yi at night and feel the particular quality of unease that comes with those curves and that darkness, you're in contact with a layered history that your body knows even if your conscious mind doesn't.

The 好兄弟 who died on these roads are part of that history. The Atayal understanding of the mountains as inhabited landscape is part of that history. The engineering decisions made without ceremony, the land taken without acknowledgment, the deaths that happened too fast and too far from family for proper rites — all of it is part of what these roads carry.

The locals who refuse to drive them at night aren't being irrational. They're being accurate.

Chapter Two

The Temples That Aren't for Praying

In Tainan's Anping District (安平區), not far from the reconstructed Dutch-era Fort Zeelandia that now functions as a tourist site, there is a small temple that most guidebooks don't mention. It doesn't have an impressive facade or famous deity statues. What it has is a list of names — hundreds of them, brushed onto wooden tablets — and a particular category of spiritual work that it has been quietly doing for over a century.

This is a 陰廟 (yīn miào). The character 陰 (yīn) refers to the yin principle — darkness, the feminine, the moon, and in this context, the realm of the dead. An 陰廟 is a temple not for the living to petition the great deities, but for the living to maintain their obligations to the unworshipped dead.

To understand why these temples exist, you need to understand one of the foundational anxieties of Taiwanese religious life: the 孤魂野鬼 (gū hún yě guǐ), the "lone souls and wild ghosts." These are the spirits of people who died without family to perform the proper rites — travelers, soldiers, migrants, victims of disaster or violence — whose souls have no anchor in the world of the dead because no one is burning offerings for them, no one is maintaining the relationship. In Taiwanese religious cosmology, an unanchored spirit doesn't simply disappear. It persists, increasingly desperate, capable of causing harm not from malice exactly, but from the same kind of desperate need that drives a starving person to steal.
