Foreword: The Island That Remembers

Imagine stepping onto an island where the ground itself feels heavier than it should. Not because of the weight of the mountains or the density of the jungles, but because this place has been asked to hold too much for too long.

Taiwan sits at the edge of the Philippine Sea Plate and the Eurasian Plate, a restless boundary where the earth constantly pushes and folds against itself. Earthquakes are not interruptions here; they are reminders that the land is still forming. But the tremors you feel when you stand in certain quiet spots are not always coming from below the surface. Sometimes they rise from layers of memory that the island has been forced to carry: waves of settlers, colonizers, soldiers, refugees, and the countless lives that ended abruptly along its roads, in its waters, and among its hills.

This is not a haunted island in the way ghost stories are usually told. It is an island that remembers.

Throughout its history, Taiwan has seen regimes rise and fall with a frequency that leaves marks. Indigenous peoples lived here for thousands of years before waves of Han Chinese migrants arrived, followed by Dutch, Spanish, Qing, Japanese, and then the Nationalist government after 1949. Each shift brought new stories, new burials, new reasons for the living to speak softly in certain places.

Foreign visitors often remark on Taiwan's breathtaking landscapes—the steep green mountains dropping into the Pacific, the misty tea plantations, the vibrant night markets. What they rarely sense at first is the quiet understanding among locals that some of these places carry something else. Not evil, not necessarily dangerous in a dramatic sense, but different. A weight. A presence shaped by geography that refuses to forget, history that refuses to smooth itself over, and beliefs that treat the land as a living participant rather than a backdrop.

In the chapters that follow, we will walk through specific places where this feeling becomes most tangible. We will not chase cheap thrills or invent shadows. Instead, we will look at how Taiwan's people have learned to live alongside these landscapes—speaking to them, respecting their rules, and acknowledging that the island was always different because it had no choice but to be.

Come with an open mind. Leave your assumptions about what "haunted" means at the door. This is a conversation between a curious outsider and a place that has seen too many outsiders come and go without ever truly listening.

Chapter 1: The Mountain Roads That Locals Don't Drive at Night

Some roads in Taiwan are not just routes from one place to another. They are passages where the living and the remembered travel together.

If you ask a Taiwanese driver why they avoid certain mountain roads after dark, you will rarely get a direct answer about ghosts. Instead, they might say something practical: the curves are sharp, the fog comes down quickly, and it's easy to make a mistake. But listen longer, and the real reason emerges—not fear of crashing, but respect for what the road has already taken.

Take the Beiyi Highway (北寺公路), the old route connecting Taipei to Yilan County. With its famous "nine bends and eighteen turns," this road earned the nickname "Highway of Death" long before any supernatural tales attached themselves to it. Poor lighting, steep drops, and frequent accidents made it notorious. Drivers used to scatter ghost money—joss paper burned for the deceased—along the roadside to appease those who never made it home. Even today, as the road has improved and become popular with motorcyclists, the collective memory lingers.

Similar stories cling to the Suhua Highway (被講公路), the narrow, cliff-hugging route along Taiwan's rugged east coast between Suao and Hualien. For decades, this was one of the most dangerous roads in the world: single-lane sections with no guardrails in places, constant threat of falling rocks, and sheer drops into the Pacific. Construction and maintenance claimed many lives, and typhoons have repeatedly turned sections into graveyards. Memorials to workers and victims still stand quietly beside the modern improvements.

These are not random tragedies. Taiwan's central mountain range is young, geologically restless, and prone to landslides. The combination of heavy rainfall, steep terrain, and human determination to connect remote areas has created roads that feel almost alive with the consequences of that ambition. But the cultural layer goes deeper.

Among the Atayal (泉閊族) people, one of Taiwan's Indigenous groups with deep roots in the northern and central mountains, mountains hold sacred power and strict boundaries. The Atayal concept of utux—ancestral spirits—guides daily life through gaga, the ancestral teachings and taboos passed down through generations. Certain areas in the high mountains are approached with caution because they belong to the spirits; disturbing them without proper respect can bring misfortune. While not every driver today identifies as Atayal, these older ways of relating to the land have quietly shaped broader Taiwanese attitudes toward mountain spaces: you do not treat them casually.

This is why you might hear a taxi driver on a lonely mountain road talking to himself—or seemingly to someone else. It is not eccentricity. In Taiwanese folk practice, speaking aloud can serve as a gentle announcement to any unseen presences: "I'm just passing through. I mean no harm. Please let me continue safely." It is a small act of politeness toward the hǎo xiōngdì (好伙御), the "good brothers"—a respectful term for wandering or unclaimed spirits who might linger where sudden deaths occurred.

Locals will tell you that accidents on these roads often cluster in ways that feel more than statistical. A curve where multiple vehicles have gone over the edge becomes a place where drivers instinctively slow down, honk their horns, or turn on interior lights. Not out of superstition alone, but from a shared understanding that the land here has absorbed too many stories too quickly.

Yet these roads are also breathtaking. The Beiyi Highway winds through mist-shrouded forests that feel timeless. The Suhua offers views of marble cliffs plunging into turquoise water that can take your breath away. The tension between beauty and unease is part of what makes them distinctly Taiwanese. The landscape does not hide its history; it wears it openly, and locals have learned to navigate both the physical curves and the invisible ones.

When you next ride through Taiwan's mountains at dusk, notice how the conversation in the car quiets. Notice how the driver might reach for the radio or start humming. It is not fear exactly. It is acknowledgment—that this place was always different, and the respectful way to move through it is to remember that you are only a temporary guest on roads that have carried far more than cars and travelers.

Chapter 2: The Temples That Aren't for Praying

Not every temple in Taiwan is a house of gods. Some are quiet shelters for those the living world has forgotten.

Walk down almost any older street in Taiwan and you might pass a small, unassuming structure with red lanterns, incense holders, and offerings of food and ghost money. At first glance, it looks like any other neighborhood shrine. But look closer—no grand statues of popular deities like Mazu or Guanyin dominate the altar. Instead, you may see simple tablets, stones, or even just a quiet space dedicated to hǎo xiōngdì—the good brothers, the unclaimed souls who died without proper rites or family to remember them.

These are yin miao (阴店), often translated as "ghost temples" or "yin temples." Unlike regular temples that worship gods (shén), yin temples primarily honor wandering spirits, particularly those who became gūhún yěguǐ (孨髸野鬼)—lonely souls and wild ghosts—after dying in accidents, wars, or disasters far from home. Taiwan's history of migration, conflict, and natural calamities has left many such souls, and the culture developed a practical, compassionate response: give them a place, feed them, and ask them to keep the peace.

The logic is straightforward and deeply rooted in Taiwanese folk belief. If these spirits have nowhere to go and nothing to sustain them, they may cause trouble out of hunger or resentment. By building Wan Shan Tang (萬善堂, Halls of Ten Thousand Good Deeds) or Da Zhong Miao (大盦店, Temples for the Masses), communities offer ongoing care. In return, the spirits are expected to protect the area rather than disturb it. It is a social contract extended to the unseen.

Consider the Laodagong Temple (老大公店) in Keelung, one of the better-known yin miao. It honors those who died in the violent clashes between early Quanzhou and Zhangzhou settlers during the Qing period—conflicts that left many bodies unclaimed along the northern coast. The temple does not boast ornate carvings or gilded roofs; it is modest, almost humble, with altars laid out for the collective dead. Locals visit not only during festivals but quietly throughout the year, leaving plates of simple food, cigarettes, or small cups of wine. The understanding is that these "good brothers" appreciate being remembered as ordinary people once were, not elevated into distant gods.

The temple becomes a quiet exchange: respect given, protection quietly received.

Rules for visiting are passed down through everyday conversation rather than written signs. You do not enter casually at night. You do not make loud requests for wealth or love without genuine need. Offerings should match what a hungry traveler might want—rice, fruit, meat dishes on festival days. Burning ghost money is common, but the amount and manner matter; it is done with care, never scattered carelessly. And if a request is fulfilled—perhaps a lost item returns or a difficult situation resolves—you return promptly with thanks, often in the form of a public feast or additional donations to the temple's upkeep. To forget this step is to invite imbalance.

This practice grew from Taiwan's particular history. Waves of migrants crossed the treacherous Taiwan Strait, many never reaching shore. Later came wars, floods, mining collapses, and the upheaval of 1949. Each event left unsettled spirits. Rather than ignore them, communities chose inclusion. Wan Shan Tang became a practical solution: collective care for the collective dead. In modern times, even as cities expand and old neighborhoods disappear, these temples remain, often relocated rather than demolished, because the understanding persists that the land still carries those stories.

During Guǐ Yuè (鬼月, Ghost Month), the seventh lunar month, activity around yin miao increases visibly. Families bring larger offerings and perform simple rituals to ensure the good brothers do not wander too far. It is not spectacle; it is maintenance of the social fabric that includes the unseen.

These temples reveal something gentle at the heart of Taiwanese belief: a refusal to abandon anyone, even after death. In a place shaped by so many arrivals and departures, the living have learned that peace comes from making room for everyone who passed through. The small red lanterns outside a yin miao are not warnings. They are invitations to rest.

When you next notice one of these quiet structures tucked between apartment blocks or beside a busy road, pause for a moment. Simply recognize that here, in this ordinary-looking spot, Taiwan quietly honors those who have no other home.

Chapter 3: The Old Neighborhoods That Push You Out

Some streets do not welcome newcomers easily. The houses seem to remember too much, and the feeling of being gently but firmly encouraged to leave is unmistakable.

In Taiwan, the concept of a xiong zhai (凶宅)—a house associated with unnatural death—carries real weight in daily life. It is not mere folklore. Since the early 2010s, real estate law has required sellers to disclose in official transaction documents whether a property has seen murder, suicide, or other non-natural deaths during the seller's period of ownership. The rule exists because buyers and neighbors have long sensed something off in certain homes: a persistent chill, unexplained noises, or a feeling that the space itself resists new occupants. The disclosure law simply formalizes what Taiwanese people have quietly practiced for generations.

This sensitivity runs deepest in older neighborhoods where history layers thickly. Take Jiufen (丢份) and the surrounding Ruifang area in New Taipei. Once a bustling gold-mining town during the Japanese colonial period and into the mid-20th century, Jiufen's narrow alleyways and hillside homes absorbed the stories of sudden wealth, cave-ins, and the quiet departures of miners who never returned. Many of the wooden and brick houses that remain were built quickly to house laborers. When the mines declined, some stood empty for years. Locals will tell you that certain lanes feel heavier after dusk, not because of any dramatic haunting, but because the collective memory of hardship and loss lingers in the very layout of the streets.

The same quiet unease appears in Taiwan's juancun (砒村, military dependents' villages) built in the 1950s to house soldiers who retreated from the mainland in 1949 and their families. These compact communities—rows of simple, uniform houses with shared courtyards—became microcosms of displacement. Entire families arrived carrying little more than the clothes on their backs and memories of homes left behind. Life inside the juancun was marked by rationing, shared loss, and the long wait for a return that never came. Many of these villages have since been demolished or transformed into cultural sites, but the ones that remain still carry a particular atmosphere. The walls seem to hold the weight of unfinished conversations between generations separated by the Taiwan Strait.

The house does not want to be forgotten; it simply asks to be left as it is.

What outsiders might call superstition is, for many Taiwanese, a practical response to rapid change. Urbanization moved at breathtaking speed after the 1960s. Old neighborhoods were cleared for high-rises, leaving behind pockets of older homes whose stories were never fully resolved. When a property becomes known as xiong zhai—perhaps after a suicide or tragic accident—future tenants often report unease within weeks of moving in. Neighbors might quietly advise against renting or buying, not with dramatic warnings but with a simple shrug and the words, "That place has its own history." Real estate agents have learned to note these details upfront because the market itself enforces the rule: such properties sit longer on the market and sell at a discount.

Yet these neighborhoods are not avoided entirely. In Jiufen, tourists still wander the teahouses and souvenir shops, drawn by the nostalgic beauty of the lantern-lit lanes. In former juancun sites, families return for reunions or to show children where their grandparents once lived. The push you feel is rarely hostile. It is more like a gentle reminder that you are walking through someone else's memories.

This feeling of being "pushed out" reveals how Taiwanese communities maintain balance with their past. Rather than tear down or ignore, they respect the boundary. A house that has seen too much is allowed its solitude. The disclosure law is only the legal echo of a deeper cultural habit: living lightly in spaces that have already carried heavy burdens.

Next time you wander through an old alley in Jiufen or pass the quiet rows of a preserved juancun, notice how your steps slow. The neighborhood is not empty. It is simply full in a way that asks you to tread carefully and perhaps not stay too long.

Chapter 4: The Water That Calls

Some bodies of water in Taiwan do not merely reflect the sky. They seem to reach out, as if asking the living to join those already resting beneath the surface.

The belief in shuǐ guǐ zhuā jiāotì (水鬼敭交替)—water ghosts seeking replacements—runs through Taiwanese waterside communities like an undercurrent. According to the tradition, those who drown become trapped in the water until they find another person to take their place. Only then can they move on to reincarnation. It is not a tale of random malice. It is a cultural explanation for why certain drownings seem to follow patterns, claiming lives in the same river bend or beach cove year after year.

The Tamsui River (焔水河), flowing past Taipei into the Taiwan Strait, has long been associated with this belief. For centuries, bodies have drifted downstream after floods or accidents. The river's strong currents and sudden tides claimed many during the era of sailboats and early ferries. Locals along its banks still speak softly of nights when the water seems to whisper. Swimmers are warned never to venture in alone after dark, and parents teach children not to call out names near the edge—doing so, folklore says, might invite the water ghost to answer in your voice.

Similar caution surrounds mountain streams across the island. The Dabaoxi stream in New Taipei's Sanxia area is known for its treacherous currents after rain. Stories circulate of survivors who felt hands grasping their ankles underwater. These accounts are not presented as horror but as reminders of the need for respect. The water here has taken too many already, and the community responds by maintaining small shrines or simple stone markers near popular entry points.

The cultural explanation goes beyond fear of accidents. Taiwan's geography makes water both life-giving and dangerous: steep mountains funnel heavy rains into flash floods, typhoons lash the coasts, and rivers change course unpredictably. In earlier times, when bridges were few and boats the main transport, drownings were common among migrants and laborers. The shuǐ guǐ legend offered a way to make sense of repeated loss—through avoidance during Guǐ Yuè, through group swimming rather than solo, and through quiet offerings of fruit or incense at river mouths to calm the unseen.

The call is not always loud. Sometimes it is only the sudden silence when you stand too close to the edge.

Taboos are practical. Do not swim at night or during Ghost Month. If someone is pulled under, rescuers are taught to extend a branch or rope from the bank rather than grabbing directly. Even today, lifeguards at popular beaches quietly reinforce these rules during peak seasons.

Yet the water is also beautiful. The same rivers that carry these stories feed rice fields and provide scenic backdrops for hiking trails. The tension between allure and respect is part of daily life for those who live near them. Parents tell children the stories not to frighten but to teach that the landscape is alive and must be approached with care.

In the end, the water that calls reflects Taiwan's relationship with its environment. The island's rivers and seas have witnessed too many crossings, too many sudden endings. The belief in water ghosts is a way of acknowledging that some debts to the water can never be fully repaid—only honored by living alongside them wisely.

Chapter 5: Places That Appeared on No Map

Some locations in Taiwan were never meant to be forgotten. They simply slipped off the maps when the world moved on, leaving behind structures that still feel occupied.

Taiwan's abandoned places carry a particular sensitivity because they represent moments when history accelerated and then abruptly stopped. Mines closed, industries shifted, colonial projects were abandoned mid-stride. The empty buildings left behind absorbed the lives that once filled them—miners, patients, soldiers—and now stand as quiet witnesses.

The most striking example is the 13-Layer Remains (十三層遺壔) in Jinguashi, part of the Ruifang District in New Taipei. Built by the Japanese during the colonial era as a massive copper smelter, the stepped concrete structure clings to the mountainside like a forgotten fortress overlooking the Pacific. When global copper prices collapsed in the 1980s, the mine and refinery shut down almost overnight. Workers left, families followed, and the entire complex was simply locked and abandoned. Today it looms over the Gold Museum area, its rusting pipes and empty halls visited only by hikers and photographers. Locals will tell you the place feels watched, especially toward evening, as if the spirits of the miners who once labored there are still checking the furnaces.

Other abandoned Japanese-era buildings scattered across the island evoke the same response. Old hospitals and sanatoriums from the colonial period, many constructed for workers or military use, were left behind after 1945 or later economic shifts. Their long corridors and peeling walls seem to echo with footsteps that never quite fade. Taiwan's rapid modernization after the 1960s meant many such structures were bypassed rather than demolished—too costly to tear down, too far from new development. The result is pockets of stillness where time feels suspended.

These places did not die. They were simply left mid-sentence.

The sensitivity to abandoned sites stems from Taiwan's particular history of boom and bust. Mining towns like Jinguashi thrived on gold and copper, drawing laborers from across the island and beyond. When the resources ran out, entire communities emptied within months. The buildings that remained held the unfinished stories of those who had invested their lives there. Taiwanese culture, shaped by repeated waves of arrival and departure, developed a quiet wariness of such places. Entering one without care feels like disturbing a conversation still in progress.

Yet these sites are also part of the landscape's beauty. The 13-Layer Remains at sunset, with the sea glinting below and mist rising from the valley, is undeniably striking. The pull is the same tension found elsewhere on the island: breathtaking views layered with the knowledge that the ground beneath has carried more than its share of human effort and loss.

Modern visitors are drawn to these places for their atmosphere, but locals approach them with understated respect. A quick bow or a few words spoken aloud before moving on. The understanding is that these spots exist outside ordinary time. They appeared on no current map because the living world moved forward, but the land itself has not finished remembering.

In this way, Taiwan's abandoned places complete the picture. They show that the island does not erase its past. It simply sets some chapters aside, waiting for those who know how to read them quietly.

Closing: Why This Place Stays With You

You leave Taiwan carrying more than souvenirs or photographs. Something about the island settles quietly inside you, a feeling that the landscapes you walked through were never just scenery. They were participants in a long conversation between earth, history, and the people who learned to live with both.

It is not the dramatic ghosts of movies that linger. It is the everyday acknowledgments: a driver speaking softly on a mountain road, a small offering left at a yin miao, the instinctive pause before entering an old neighborhood lane. These gestures show a culture that has made peace with a land asked to hold too much.

Taiwan does not ask you to believe in the unseen. It only asks you to notice that the seen has never been alone.

That is why the place stays with you. The mountains, the old streets, the rivers, and the quiet temples are not backdrops. They are the record of everyone who ever passed through. When you return home and find yourself thinking of a particular curve on the Suhua Highway or the way mist gathers around the 13-Layer Remains, you are not remembering a vacation. You are remembering a place that remembers.

And perhaps, in some small way, you now carry a piece of that quiet understanding: some places were always different, and the respectful thing is simply to listen.
